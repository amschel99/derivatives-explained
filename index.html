Linear vs Non-Linear Derivatives: Understanding Delta and Gamma from Scratch

A simple guide built from rise-over-run and a small dose of calculus.

Most explanations of options Greeks start with formulas. This one starts with a question you already know how to answer: what's the slope of a line?

By the end, you'll understand delta and gamma — the two quantities that every options trader watches all day — using nothing more than the rise-over-run trick you learned in school, plus one small extension.

What is a derivative?

A derivative is a contract whose value depends on something else — the "underlying." The contract itself isn't the thing; its value just rides on whatever happens to the thing. A bet on a football match isn't the match, but its value depends entirely on what happens in it.

For this whole article, the underlying will be one fictional stock: TechCo, currently trading at $10. We'll look at two kinds of bets on TechCo and compare how they behave.

  • Bet 1 — Futures contract (linear): You agree to buy TechCo at $10. Whatever the price ends up at, you pocket the gain or eat the loss.
  • Bet 2 — Call option (non-linear): You pay $1 today for the right (not obligation) to buy TechCo at $12 later. If the stock stays below $12, you walk away — your $1 is gone. If it goes above $12, you make the difference minus the $1 you already paid.

Part 1: The linear bet (futures)

The PnL formula

For a long futures contract bought at entry price F0:

PnL = S − F0

where S is the current TechCo price.

  • If S = 13 and F0 = 10: PnL = 13 − 10 = +$3 (gain)
  • If S = 7 and F0 = 10: PnL = 7 − 10 = −$3 (loss)

The minus sign matters — it's the difference between making and losing money. Don't take an absolute value, you'd throw that information away.

If you plot this — PnL on the y-axis, stock price S on the x-axis — you get a perfectly straight line tilted upward.

Calculating the slope (rise over run)

Pick any two points on the line:

slope = (change in PnL) / (change in S)

Pick S = 12 and S = 13:

  • At S = 12, PnL = $2
  • At S = 13, PnL = $3
slope = (2 − 3) / (12 − 13) = −1 / −1 = 1

Or going the other direction (same answer, cleaner-looking):

slope = (3 − 2) / (13 − 12) = 1 / 1 = 1

Try any two points along the line — slope is always 1. That's what "linear" means: the slope doesn't depend on where you measure it.

What is delta?

Delta = the slope of the PnL curve.

For futures, delta is always 1. In plain English: "If the stock moves up by $1, my PnL moves up by exactly $1."

What is gamma?

Gamma = the slope of the delta curve. Asks: "is delta itself changing as the stock price moves?"

For futures, delta = 1 at every price. Plot delta against S and you get a flat horizontal line at height 1. The slope of a flat line is 0.

So gamma = 0 for futures.

That's the mathematical reason futures are called linear. Their slope (delta) is constant, and the slope of their slope (gamma) is zero. Boring, predictable, and easy to reason about.

Part 2: The non-linear bet (call option)

The PnL formula at expiration

For a long call option (strike K, premium paid P), at the moment the option expires:

PnL = max(0, S − K) − P

If S < K, the right to buy at K is worthless — you only lost the premium P.
If S > K, your right to buy at K is worth (S − K), so net you make (S − K) − P.

Using K = $12 and P = $1:

  • If S = $8: PnL = max(0, −4) − 1 = 0 − 1 = −$1
  • If S = $14: PnL = max(0, 2) − 1 = 2 − 1 = +$1

The PnL is flat at −$1 for any price below $12 (the option just expires worthless and you've lost your $1 premium). Then at $12 it bends and starts rising.

That bend is what makes it non-linear. Plot it and you get a hockey-stick shape, not a straight line.

Why the curve is more interesting before expiration

The kinked hockey-stick is the picture exactly at expiration. Before that — when there's still time for the stock to move — the option's value is a smooth curve that hugs the kinked payoff but rounds off the corner. The math for that smooth curve is called the Black-Scholes formula. You don't need to know the formula. Just trust that we can get a value at any stock price.

We'll use these smooth values for the delta and gamma calculations below.

The slope problem

Here's the issue with a curve: the slope is different at every point.

  • At S = $7, the curve is almost flat → slope near 0
  • At S = $12, the curve is bending the hardest → slope ≈ 0.5
  • At S = $20, the curve is nearly diagonal → slope near 1

So you can't just pick any two points and say "the slope is X." You'll get a different X depending on which points you pick. That's a problem we have to solve.

Part 3: How to find the slope at one specific point (delta)

The trick — shrink the gap

You still need 2 points to do rise-over-run. So:

  1. Pick the point you care about (let's say S = $13).
  2. Pick a second point right next to it.
  3. Compute rise/run.
  4. Move the second point closer.
  5. Compute rise/run again.
  6. Keep moving it closer. The numbers will settle on one value.

That settled value is the slope at your point.

Easy practice on a familiar curve: y = x² at x = 3

Let's forget options for a minute and try this on the simplest curve everyone knows: y = x².

At x = 3, y = 9 (because 3 × 3 = 9). That's our anchor.

What 0.66 means in plain English

"If TechCo goes up by $1, the option's value goes up by about 66 cents."

Compare to the futures:

  • Futures delta = 1 → stock up $1 → PnL up $1 (full move)
  • Option delta at $13 = 0.66 → stock up $1 → option up only $0.66 (partial move)

The option moves less because at $13 we're sitting on the bend of the curve, not its steep part. If we redid this exercise at S = $20, option delta would be near 1 (option behaves almost exactly like a future). If we redid it at S = $7, delta would be near 0 (option barely reacts to stock moves).

That's the whole story of non-linear: delta isn't a single number — it's a different number at every price.

Part 4: How to find gamma

Gamma = the slope of the delta curve.

So we use the same rise-over-run trick again — but on delta values instead of option values.

Get delta at several prices near $13

Same Black-Scholes machine, just spitting out delta this time:

Stock price SDelta
13.0000.6644
13.5000.7115
13.1000.6742
13.0100.6654

Rise/run on the delta values

Anchor at S = 13. Pick a second price. Rise/run on the delta numbers, then shrink the gap:

PairRise / RunSlope
13 → 13.5(0.7115 − 0.6644) / 0.50.094
13 → 13.1(0.6742 − 0.6644) / 0.10.098
13 → 13.01(0.6654 − 0.6644) / 0.010.10

Slopes settle at about 0.099. That's gamma at S = $13.

What 0.099 means in plain English

"If TechCo goes up by $1, delta itself goes up by about 0.10."

Watch the cascade this creates:

  • At $13, delta = 0.66 → option moves $0.66 per $1 of stock
  • At $14, delta ≈ 0.66 + 0.10 = 0.76 → option moves $0.76 per $1 of stock
  • At $15, delta ≈ 0.76 + 0.09 ≈ 0.85 → option moves $0.85 per $1 of stock

The option becomes more and more responsive as the stock climbs. That accelerating sensitivity is gamma. It's why option traders call gamma "convexity" — the curve is bending in your favor as the stock moves.

Part 5: Putting it all together

You just learned a powerful idea: the same rise-over-run-and-shrink trick gives you both delta and gamma.

  1. First time — apply the trick to the option value curve → get delta (the slope of option value).
  2. Second time — apply the trick to the delta curve → get gamma (the slope of delta).

That's all "second derivative" means in calculus. Do the slope trick twice. The first slope is speed; the second is acceleration.

The whole article in two sentences

For a linear bet, PnL is a straight line — its slope is the same everywhere, so delta is constant and gamma is zero.

For a non-linear bet, PnL is a curve — its slope changes as the underlying moves (that's delta as a function of S), and the slope of that slope is gamma.

Both are computed the same way: pick two points, rise over run, shrink the gap until the answer stops changing.

Calculus is just rise-over-run with a magnifying glass.

A small bonus — what comes after gamma?

If you apply the trick a third time, on the gamma values themselves, you get a quantity called speed — how fast gamma itself is changing. Apply it a fourth time and you get color. Almost no one uses these in practice, but the procedure is identical: rise/run, shrink the gap. The whole tower of derivatives — first, second, third, fourth — is built from the same one move.

That's the secret. One move, applied over and over.
